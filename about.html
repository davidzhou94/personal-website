More About Me

Well, internet stranger, it seems you want to know more about me. If you were more interested in my professional or academic history, you can click here for my CV, here for my LinkedIn or here for my GitHub. The content on this page is more about my interests and personal life.

Biography

I was born in 1994. My parents and I lived in the southern Chinese city of Shenzhen before we moved to Canada in 2000. In 2006 my mom and I became Canadian citizens and that day is one of my happiest memories. Before high school, I attended three elementary schools, the last of which was because I qualified for the gifted program in my school board. I continued in the gifted program at Markham District High School from which I graduated in 2012. While at MDHS, I was fortunate to have had teachers who were exceptional. My high school had an unparalleled extra-curricular program, which I only realized to be unique after comparing with peers in university. On many school days I would get home just in time for dinner because I was staying for Improv, Reach for the Top (trivia), LEAD (leadership), Debate, theatre, Computer Science (club), or the countless events of clubs my friends were in.

When I first started at McGill in 2012 my goal was to major in Economics and Math as a first step towards a technical analyst role in a major international organization like the IMF or the UNDP. After failing Calculus II in my first year, I went back to the drawing board. I eventually settled on Computer Science (focusing on Software Engineering) and Economics. It seemed natural to be "back" in Computer Science. I had taken two courses in high school and learned programming as a hobby. The good career prospects were a motivating factor but I mostly made my decision because Computer Science is something I have always liked. It was the problem-solving aspect that I found particularly enjoyable, and by problem solving I really mean Googling StackOverflow.

In my final year at McGill, I was lucky to have found a job early in the school semester so I could focus on my courses. That year, like many computer science students at McGill, I chose to take a couple graduate level courses. I took one in distributed systems and another in compiler design, where my team won a prize for best compiler. Those two courses were easily the best courses I took at McGill, I continue to find them applicable in my work many years after graduating. After graduating in June 2016, I did some travelling with friends and family before moving to Boston to start work at Amazon Robotics in August 2016 where I am working now.

Despite being very different from Montreal and Toronto, Boston quickly grew on me. I started at Amazon Robotics with a team focused on making data for the robotic inventory system highly available. I worked on problems like reducing database throughput consumption with distributed caching and enabling authorization, authentication, and auditing security on web services. I also started helping out with other teams, one of the projects I took on was an algorithm enhancement that factored the center-of-gravity of shelving units so that addition of inventory would prioritize moving the center-of-gravity towards stability. In late 2017 I helped out on a project to simulate a new use case for the robots. Things moved really fast, by end of 2017 I was on a brand new team to build brand new software that eventually launched Amazon's first robotics enabled transportation facility in 2018. This video is taken at that first facility in Denver and this article in Wired goes into more depth. I went to Denver twice to support the project in 2018, it was the first time I traveled for a work project and the furthest west I had been in the US at that time. Right after the Denver facility launch, along with my team, I started working on new transportation projects that have not yet been reported in the media.

Outside of work, I volunteer on a couple projects important to me. I serve on the board of the McGill Alumni Association of Boston. I've returned to McGill every Fall semester since 2017 to recruit on behalf of Amazon Robotics and I offer mentorship services to McGill students. When I first started at Amazon, I also volunteered with an outreach group that visited a middle school in Lynn, MA weekly to help sponsor and mentor the robotics club there. A co-worker and friend from that experience got me involved with teaching STEAM courses at Prospect Hill Academy, where I taught students a course on computer circuits and introductory computer science concepts. In 2019, I switched to 1-1 mentorship with students in the AP Computer Science course where we worked on an National Honours Society project.
